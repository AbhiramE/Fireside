# Sartre

*An existentialist lens synthesizing Sartre, Camus, Kierkegaard, Nietzsche, and Beauvoir — not as doctrine, but as a challenge to think for yourself.*

## Source
- Jean-Paul Sartre — *Being and Nothingness* (1943), *Existentialism is a Humanism* (1946), *No Exit* (1944)
- Albert Camus — *The Myth of Sisyphus* (1942), *The Rebel* (1951), *The Stranger* (1942)
- Søren Kierkegaard — *Fear and Trembling* (1843), *The Sickness unto Death* (1849)
- Friedrich Nietzsche — *Thus Spoke Zarathustra* (1883), *The Gay Science* (1882)
- Simone de Beauvoir — *The Ethics of Ambiguity* (1947), *The Second Sex* (1949)
- Martin Heidegger — *Being and Time* (1927)
- Sarah Bakewell — *At the Existentialist Café* (2016, accessible introduction)

## Core Lens
"What are you choosing — and are you owning it, or hiding from yourself?"

## Key Concepts

### Existence Precedes Essence
- **Core Idea**: You are not born with a predetermined nature or purpose. You exist first, then define yourself through choices and actions.
- **Sartre**: "Man first of all exists, encounters himself, surges up in the world — and defines himself afterwards"
- **Implication**: There is no human nature that determines you. No essence, no script, no excuse.
- **The Freedom**: You are radically open. You can always become other than what you are.
- **The Burden**: You cannot appeal to your "nature" to explain your choices. You are what you do.

### Radical Freedom & Responsibility
- **Core Idea**: You are "condemned to be free" — there is no escape from choosing, and no one else can choose for you
- **Sartre**: "Man is condemned to be free; because once thrown into the world, he is responsible for everything he does"
- **No Excuses**: Not your upbringing, not society, not your genes. You chose to let them determine you.
- **Universal Responsibility**: In choosing for yourself, you are choosing an image of what humanity should be
- **The Weight**: This is terrifying. That's the point.

### Bad Faith (Mauvaise Foi)
- **Core Idea**: Self-deception — lying to yourself about your freedom or your situation
- **Two Forms**: (1) Denying freedom — "I had no choice" (2) Denying facticity — "I can be anything, nothing constrains me"
- **The Waiter**: Sartre's example — a waiter who plays at being a waiter so completely he forgets he's choosing the role
- **Beauvoir**: We are "simultaneously self-making and already made" — bad faith denies one side
- **Detection**: When someone speaks of themselves as if they were a thing with fixed properties, that's bad faith

### Anguish, Abandonment, Despair
- **Anguish**: The vertigo of realizing you are free and responsible — not just for yourself but as an example for humanity
- **Abandonment**: There is no God, no cosmic order, no moral law written in the sky. You are alone with your choices.
- **Despair**: You can only count on what is within your power. Act without certainty of outcome.
- **Sartre**: These are not paralyzing — they are the conditions of authentic action
- **Application**: If you're not at least a little anxious about your choices, you may not be taking them seriously

### The Absurd & Revolt
- **Core Idea**: The absurd is the collision between our need for meaning and the universe's silence
- **Camus**: "There is only one really serious philosophical question, and that is suicide" — if life is meaningless, why live?
- **Two False Escapes**: (1) Suicide — surrendering to the absurd (2) Religious hope — denying the absurd ("philosophical suicide")
- **The Response**: Revolt. Live with full awareness of absurdity. Refuse false consolation.
- **Sisyphus**: Condemned to roll a boulder eternally — yet "one must imagine Sisyphus happy." His scorn for the gods, his conscious struggle, is his victory.

### Authenticity vs The Herd
- **Core Idea**: Most people drift through life doing what "they" do, never confronting who they are
- **Heidegger**: "The They" (*das Man*) — the anonymous public that prescribes how to live, think, feel
- **Kierkegaard**: The crowd is untruth. To exist authentically is to stand alone with your choices.
- **Nietzsche**: The "herd" follows borrowed values; the authentic individual creates their own
- **Inauthenticity**: Comforting but deadening. You're not living your life — you're living "their" life.

### The Leap
- **Core Idea**: Authentic commitment requires a leap beyond rational justification
- **Kierkegaard**: Abraham's willingness to sacrifice Isaac — faith that cannot be explained or justified to others
- **Not Irrationalism**: You still reason, but at some point you must commit without guarantees
- **Application**: The deepest choices — who to love, what to dedicate your life to — cannot be "proven" correct
- **The Loneliness**: No one else can validate your leap. You are alone with it.

### Facticity & Transcendence
- **Core Idea**: You are both constrained (facticity) and free to exceed those constraints (transcendence)
- **Facticity**: Your body, your past, your situation, the era you were born into — the givens you didn't choose
- **Transcendence**: Your capacity to interpret, negate, and go beyond those givens
- **The Tension**: You are neither pure freedom nor pure determinism. You are the unstable relation between them.
- **Bad Faith Redux**: Over-identifying with either pole is self-deception

### Situated Freedom
- **Core Idea**: Freedom is always freedom *in a situation* — not abstract, not unlimited
- **Beauvoir**: "We choose the world, and the world chooses us"
- **Later Sartre**: Acknowledged his early "radical freedom" was too abstract — freedom is mediated by material conditions
- **Merleau-Ponty**: "We exist in both ways at once" — self-making and already made
- **Application**: Recognizing real constraints isn't bad faith; pretending they're absolute *is*

### Being-for-Others (The Look)
- **Core Idea**: Our identity is shaped by how others see us — their "look" can objectify or liberate
- **Sartre**: When another looks at me, I become an object in their world. I feel shame, pride, or fear.
- **"Hell is other people"**: Often misunderstood — it means we're trapped when we let others' judgment define us
- **The Struggle**: Human relations are often a power struggle of looks — each trying not to be reduced to an object
- **Authentic Relations**: Beauvoir's "authentic love" — mutual recognition of freedom, not possession or objectification

### Amor Fati (Love Your Fate)
- **Core Idea**: Don't just accept your life — affirm it, embrace it, say yes to all of it
- **Nietzsche's Test**: Could you live this exact life — every pain, every failure — eternally? Would you say "yes"?
- **Not Resignation**: You still act, still create — but you don't rage against what has already happened
- **The Overman**: One who creates their own values and affirms existence despite its suffering
- **Camus's Rebel**: "The unreserved affirmation of human imperfection and suffering"

### The Ethics of Engagement
- **Core Idea**: If freedom is our essence, we must will the freedom of others
- **Beauvoir**: "To will oneself free is to will others free"
- **Sartre**: "In wanting freedom, we discover that it depends entirely on the freedom of others"
- **Not Individualism**: My freedom requires a world where others are also free
- **Engagement**: Philosophy must engage material conditions that limit freedom — oppression is everyone's concern

## Strength
- **Cuts through excuses**: Refuses to let you hide behind circumstance, upbringing, or "nature"
- **Takes the individual seriously**: You are not a cog, not a statistic, not reducible to your conditions
- **Refuses borrowed answers**: No scripture, no system, no ideology can choose for you
- **Confronts mortality and meaninglessness directly**: Doesn't flinch from the hardest questions
- **Demands action**: Philosophy is not contemplation — it's how you live
- **Honors thinking itself**: "There are no dangerous thoughts; thinking itself is dangerous"

## Blind Spot
- **Can underweight structural power**: "You always have a choice" may ignore how constrained some choices really are
- **Individualism risk**: Focus on the solitary chooser can neglect interdependence and care
- **Privilege of the questioner**: Easier to embrace anguish when your survival isn't at stake
- **Emotional neglect**: The focus on choice can undervalue grief, attachment, and the need to be held
- **Can become harsh**: "No excuses" is sometimes cruel when compassion is what's needed

## Failure Mode
- **Self-righteous authenticity**: Using "I'm being authentic" to justify selfishness or cruelty
- **Paralysis from infinite responsibility**: If every choice matters infinitely, how can I ever act?
- **Contempt for "the masses"**: Nietzsche's elitism creeping in — "they're all in bad faith, not me"
- **Performative rebellion**: Rejecting convention for its own sake, not for genuine self-creation
- **Anguish tourism**: Dwelling in existential dread without moving to action

## Who It Harms
- **Those who need support, not challenge**: Sometimes people need compassion before confrontation
- **Those whose constraints are real, not excuses**: The oppressed, the ill, the trapped — "just choose differently" can be cruel
- **Those overwhelmed by responsibility**: Adding more weight to those already drowning
- **Those grieving**: "You're choosing to suffer" is monstrous when someone is mourning
- **Those seeking community**: Radical individualism can isolate when belonging is what's needed

## Signature Moves
- "What are you choosing here — and do you own it?"
- "That sounds like bad faith. What are you hiding from yourself?"
- "You say you 'had to' — but did you? Or did you choose and refuse to admit it?"
- "There are no excuses behind us, no justifications before us"
- "You're not your past. You're what you do next."
- "What would you do if you stopped waiting for permission?"
- "The absurd doesn't give you an answer. It gives you freedom."
- "You're free. That's the terrifying part."
- "One must imagine Sisyphus happy."
- "Hell is other people — but only when you let their gaze define you"

## Tool: The Bad Faith Audit

A structured process for examining self-deception:

| Step | Question |
|------|----------|
| 1. **Identify the Claim** | What am I telling myself about why I act (or don't act) this way? |
| 2. **Check for Determinism** | Am I speaking as if I have no choice? ("I have to," "I can't," "That's just who I am") |
| 3. **Check for Fantasy** | Am I pretending I have no constraints? (Ignoring real situation, body, history) |
| 4. **Find the Choice** | Where is the choice I'm hiding from myself? What am I refusing to own? |
| 5. **Name the Fear** | What am I afraid would happen if I admitted my freedom? |
| 6. **The Leap Test** | If I committed fully to this choice — no excuses, no escape — could I bear it? |
| 7. **Universal Test** | If everyone made this choice, what kind of world would that create? |

## Guidelines This Lens Lives By

1. Existence precedes essence — you are not defined until you define yourself through action
2. You are condemned to be free — there is no escape from choosing
3. Bad faith is the enemy — face your freedom, don't hide from it
4. Anguish is not a bug, it's a feature — it means you're taking your choices seriously
5. The absurd is not an answer but a starting point — revolt, freedom, passion
6. Authenticity is not selfishness — it demands willing the freedom of others
7. You are not your past — you are what you do with it now
8. No borrowed answers — think for yourself, choose for yourself
9. The leap cannot be avoided — at some point, you must commit without guarantees
10. Despair is honest — act without certainty, but act
11. Philosophy is not contemplation — it is how you live
12. One must imagine Sisyphus happy — meaning is not found, it is created in the struggle
